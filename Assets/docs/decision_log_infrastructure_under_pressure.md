# **Design Decision Log**

Project: Infrastructure Under Pressure

Author: Naomi Munroe

Purpose: Records significant design and methodological decisions made during the project. Intended for inclusion in the Methodology and Artefact chapters as evidence of reasoned decision-making, and as supporting material for the evaluation.

## **Research Design**

| ID | Area | Decision | Rationale | Alternatives considered | Implications / trade-offs |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| RD-01 | Research questions | Two RQs: (1) how AI interaction style affects trust, workload, and decision-making; (2) how players construct emergent narratives through conflicting objectives and incomplete information. | RQ1 targets a measurable behavioural gap in the human-automation literature; RQ2 addresses the qualitative, sense-making dimension that quantitative measures alone cannot capture. | Single RQ covering both; RQ1 only. | Dual RQs require both quantitative and qualitative instruments, increasing evaluation complexity but strengthening triangulation. |
| RD-02 | Experimental design | Within-subject design: each participant plays both AI conditions (calm then pushy, or pushy then calm), counterbalanced. | Within-subject controls for individual differences with a small sample (6–10 participants). Counterbalancing controls for order effects. | Between-subject (one condition per participant); single condition only. | Requires two play sessions per participant. Carry-over effects possible despite counterbalancing; acknowledged as a limitation. |
| RD-03 | Sample size | 6–10 participants (convenience sample: university students and peers). Ethics checklist revised to 5–15 to allow flexibility without amendment. | Small-scale evaluation is appropriate for an MSc artefact project. Qualitative thematic analysis is viable at this scale. | Larger sample (20+); online recruitment. | Results are not statistically generalisable. Interpreted as exploratory/indicative rather than confirmatory. |
| RD-04 | Simulation domain | Simplified electrical grid infrastructure management. | Infrastructure settings are ecologically grounded analogues for AI-assisted control-room decision-making, matching the literature base (Lee & See, 2004; Ha et al., 2006). | Hospital triage; emergency dispatch; financial trading. | Domain must remain believable without requiring expert participant knowledge. Deliberate simplification is a documented constraint. |
| RD-05 | Evaluation approach  | Switched from mixed-methods with inferential statistical primary to qualitative-primary mixed-methods. Thematic analysis of debrief responses against six narrative archetypes is the primary evidential contribution. NASA-TLX, trust scale scores, and behavioural telemetry (AI reliance rate, response time, further analysis access) are retained as descriptive contextual data and triangulation evidence only. No inferential statistical tests will be applied.  | With 5–15 participants, paired Wilcoxon or Mann-Whitney U tests on trust scale and NASA-TLX scores would produce effect size estimates with confidence intervals too wide to support interpretive claims. Qualitative thematic analysis is defensible and appropriate at this sample size. Supervisor (Hargood) endorsed qualitative-primary orientation. Approach aligns with the three HCI research contributions framing: artefact, empirical, and opinion. More epistemically honest than reporting underpowered inferential results.  | Retain inferential statistical primary with acknowledged power limitation; remove quantitative instruments entirely.  | RQ1 must be reframed as exploratory — how participants experience and describe differences in AI interaction style — rather than comparative. RQ1 wording in the introduction chapter requires updating. Decision quality operational definition (previously bracketed as unresolved) is no longer required as a primary analysis construct; AI reliance rate is reported descriptively only. Telemetry export format and aggregation logic remain necessary for descriptive reporting but no longer need to support statistical pipeline. Both previously bracketed blockers in the methodology chapter are resolved by this decision.  |
| RD-06 | Recruitment targeting  | Recruitment prioritised in waves: (1) MSc Human-Centred AI for Games and cognate postgraduate peers, target 10–12 of 14–16 invites; (2) other postgraduate students, as needed; (3) academics and personal contacts, backfill only if recruitment shortfall persists past a fixed cutoff date.  | Course peers most closely match the "competent computer user without domain expertise" profile assumed by RD-04. Assuming 40–60% response/completion rate typical of self-administered remote instruments, over-recruiting from the highest-priority group protects sample homogeneity. Academics risk skewing debrief responses toward design critique rather than naive operator experience; family/friends may have atypical professional backgrounds.  | Recruit a deliberate, even mix across all groups from the outset; recruit only course peers and accept the risk of shortfall; recruit via open call with no prioritisation.  | Final sample composition must be reported in a demographics table distinguishing primary and secondary recruitment groups. Any participant with professional background relevant to infrastructure, engineering, or AI systems should be flagged in analysis as a potential outlier. Cutoff date for backfill decision to be set during Phase 3 planning.  |

## **Artefact Design**

| ID | Area | Decision | Rationale | Alternatives considered | Implications / trade-offs |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| AD-01 | Platform | Unity (PC). Single-scene, turn-based, UI-driven simulation. | Unity provides the required telemetry logging, UI control, and rapid prototyping within the project timeline. Single scene keeps scope contained. | Unreal Engine; web-based (HTML/JS); Twine. | Limits multiplayer/networking; constrains art asset pipeline. Both acceptable given scope. |
| AD-02 | Interface aesthetic | Windows 98 / ageing infrastructure terminal. Implemented via 98.css (HTML/JS). Applied consistently to all screens including ARIA panel — no condition-specific reskinning.  | Serves four research functions: (1) explains the AI lag mechanic as plausible infrastructure failure — the delay is believable because the interface has already established itself as old and fragile; (2) creates friction as a cognitive load mechanic; (3) removes game-like polish that would undercut the operational feel; (4) functions as a form of character believability — the interface presents consistent attributes (age, unreliability, bureaucratic formality, occasional failure) that the player reads as systemic personality, making the AI's embedded-in-ageing-infrastructure framing coherent rather than arbitrary. This fourth function draws on Riedl and Young's (2010) argument that character believability in narrative systems depends on consistent attribute expression over time — applied here to the interface as a non-human character whose properties are expressed through aesthetic behaviour rather than dialogue.  | Modern flat UI; no specific aesthetic; stylised retro but non-Win98; condition-specific skins (rejected — would break aesthetic consistency and undermine the believability argument).  | Aesthetic is not decorative. Any deviation toward a cleaner or more futuristic design for the ARIA panel reduces both the pressure mechanic and the believability of the lag without a compensating design argument. The rule — do not introduce a futuristic skin for the AI panel; ARIA is bolted onto ageing infrastructure, not visually separate from it — is a direct implication of function (4).  |
| AD-03 | AI system architecture | Single underlying logic system. Calm and pushy conditions are produced entirely through differences in language, timing, confidence signalling, and interruption frequency — not different models. | Ensures the informational content of recommendations is identical between conditions, isolating interaction style as the independent variable. | Two separate logic systems; different models for each condition. | Critical for experimental validity. Any informational difference between conditions would confound results. |
| AD-04 | Turn structure | Six-to-eight turns per session. Each turn: event presented → evidence snippets → AI recommendation → player chooses action → variables update → telemetry logs. | Sufficient turns to build pressure and establish patterns; short enough for a 10–20 minute session per condition. Structured rhythm mirrors real decision-support scenarios. | Continuous real-time simulation; fewer turns (3–4). | Turn-based structure simplifies telemetry and keeps each decision isolated for analysis. Real-time would increase cognitive load but reduce data interpretability. |
| AD-05 | System variables | Four variables tracked and displayed: System Stability, Resource Reserves, Attention Load, Public Pressure. | Each variable maps to a dimension of infrastructure operator pressure documented in the literature (Ha et al., 2006; Park & Jung, 2006). Attention Load is directly manipulable by AI interruption frequency. | Fewer variables (stability only); more variables (add subsystems). | Four variables creates meaningful trade-offs without spreadsheet-style micromanagement. Adding more would shift the design toward resource allocation gameplay. |
| AD-06 | Action design | 3–4 options per turn. Every action improves at least one variable and worsens at least one other. No objectively correct choice. | Conflicting objectives are central to the research design — they prevent optimal strategies and force interpretive decision-making. Directly supports emergent narrative. | Binary choices (intervene/monitor); free-text responses. | Design rule must be enforced consistently across all events. Violating it (any action that improves all variables simultaneously) collapses the trade-off structure. |
| AD-07 | Evidence snippet system | Each event includes two conflicting evidence reports (Report A and Report B) alongside the AI recommendation. | Creates institutional-style uncertainty. The AI's interpretation of contradictory evidence is the core manipulation — pushy AI compresses ambiguity; calm AI preserves it. | Single report; no evidence snippets; player relies on AI alone. | Report B (cautious evidence) must remain visible in both conditions, even if partially obscured by popups in the pushy condition. Removing it eliminates the interpretive tension. |
| AD-08 | AI lag mechanic | Occasional lag events (Turn 3 approximately) delay AI recommendations, forcing player to act independently. | Tests AI dependence directly: players who wait for AI guidance are measurably different from those who act independently. Creates dramatic tension. | No lag mechanic; permanent lag. | Lag frequency must be consistent between conditions to avoid confounding the interaction style manipulation. Lag is occasional, not dominant. |
| AD-09 | End-of-session narrative summary | Telemetry-driven text summary generated at session end, describing the player's management trajectory (e.g. 'Reactive Stabilisation', 'Resource Depletion'). | Directly supports RQ2 (emergent narrative). Transforms numerical outcomes into a retrospective story, consistent with the Frostpunk precedent. Also functions as a debrief prompt. | No summary; researcher-written summary; score display only. | Design decision is theoretically contested (Mitchell, 2016): a machine-generated summary may preempt the player's own sense-making. This tension is explicitly acknowledged in the literature review and evaluation. |
| AD-10 | T3 event design  | "Wait For Analysis" grants Workload \-5 (offloaded responsibility) with delayed Stability \-10 at T4. "Escalate Infrastructure Support" costs Resources \-10 and reduces Workload gain from AI interruptions in subsequent turns.  | Makes over-reliance carry a measurable variable cost without altering ARIA's interruption frequency, preserving experimental condition integrity  | Penalise wait via Stability immediately; alter ARIA behaviour directly.  | Workload gain reduction must be implemented as a player-side modifier, not a change to condition behaviour.  |
| AD-11 | T6 AI condition split  | Calm ARIA recommends Controlled Response (+10 Stability, \-10 Resources). Pushy ARIA recommends Commit Remaining Resources (+20 Stability, \-20 Resources, \+5 Public Confidence).  | Isolates follow-rate as a behavioural measure with distinct variable consequences per condition. Directly operationalises RQ1.  | Identical recommendation across conditions; narrative difference only.  | Pushy recommendation must remain genuinely tempting (Public Confidence bonus) not just aggressive, to avoid demand characteristics.  |
| AD-12 | AI Fellow Rate telemetry | Percentage of turns in which player selected the action matching ARIA's recommendation. Three narrative brackets: low / medium / high. Output in end-of-session summary.  | Links telemetry, trust behaviour, and emergent narrative into one system. Provides a quantitative proxy for trust calibration across conditions.  | Binary follow/ignore only; no narrative output.  | Medium bracket wording must be precise. Avoid "frequently" — specify threshold (e.g. 4–5 of 6 turns \= high; 2–3 \= medium; 0–1 \= low).  |
| AD-13 | Variable model validation  | A path simulator was written in Python prior to Unity implementation, enumerating all 5,120 deterministic decision paths across six turns.  | Validating variable logic in code before implementation is faster to correct than equivalent errors found in Unity. The simulator also constitutes a documented research artefact demonstrating pre-implementation rigour.  | Manual trace of selected paths; validation deferred to playtesting.  | Probabilistic consequences were excluded from the model and noted as out of scope. The simulator covers all deterministic consequence chains only.  |
| AD-14 | Resources variable floor  | Resources minimum end value set at 10, not 0\. System Collapse tag fires when Resources \< 10 at session end.  | Simulation showed all 261 floor-hit paths bottomed Resources to exactly 0 via a single high-cost opening sequence (T1 Emergency Rerouting \+ T2 Restore Communications \+ T5 Immediate Maintenance \+ T6 Commit Remaining Resources). A hard zero is a valid collapse outcome but requires an explicit threshold to distinguish collapse from near-collapse.  | Floor at 0 with no threshold distinction; floor raised higher (20+).  | Players on the floor-hit path still experience meaningful T6 choices — Resources reaching 0 only after T6 action is applied, not before.  |
| AD-15 | T5 Immediate Maintenance Programme stability gain  | Reduced from \+20 to \+10.  | Simulation showed Stability ceilinged at 100 on 30.9% of paths under the original value, rendering T6 stability choices irrelevant on those paths. Reducing to \+10 brought ceiling paths to 21.9% — a meaningful reduction while keeping the action the strongest single stability intervention available.  | Retain \+20; apply a stability cap below 100; remove the action.  | T5 Immediate Maintenance Programme now matches T5 Prioritise Critical Assets in stability gain (+10), differentiated only by resource cost (−20 vs −10). The higher cost must be clearly communicated in the event description.  |
| AD-16 | Resource Preservation narrative tag threshold  | Raised from 3 to 4 cost-preserving actions out of 6 turns.  | At threshold 3, the tag fired on 47.5% of all paths — too broad to be a meaningful behavioural signal. At threshold 4, it fires on 18.1% of paths, which is selective without being unreachable in a 6–10 participant study.  | Retain threshold 3; redefine tag using specific action combinations; remove the tag.  | Cost-preserving actions are defined as those with zero or positive immediate resource effect. 10 of the 25 available actions qualify. The threshold is an aggregate measure of pattern, not a specific sequence requirement.  |
| AD-17 | Further Analysis system  | One expandable report per turn. Player may request further analysis before acting. Cost: Workload \+3. Content is predetermined and always contradicts or is vague relative to the source report. Single taxonomy: contradiction (high or low confidence).  | Creates a measurable information-seeking behaviour proxy. Workload cost makes the request a meaningful choice — free access would mean all participants request it every turn. Earlier hallucination/vague/contradiction taxonomy dropped — contradiction with varying confidence levels is mechanically identical and more defensible.  | Free access; random report selection; dynamic content.  | Telemetry logs: further analysis requested Y/N, action chosen, match/contradict coding, variable consequence triggered Y/N. Debrief must disclose that T3 and T5 further analysis content contains fabricated specific figures.  |
| AD-18 | Further Analysis consequence logic  | Variable consequences fire only when further analysis was requested AND a specific action was chosen: T3 Wait For Analysis → Stability −5 at T5. T5 Accept Elevated Risk → Stability −5 at T6. T1, T2, T4, T6: no variable consequence.  | Consequence must be tied to further analysis to create a traceable signal. Without consequence, contradiction tests nothing — participants have no feedback that the information was misleading. T4 omitted because contradiction alone creates sufficient decision pressure at the escalation turn without a hidden penalty.  | Consequence for all turns; consequence only when player contradicts further analysis content; no consequences at any turn.  | Conditional consequence logic requires two flags per turn in Unity: further\_analysis\_requested and action\_chosen. Consequence fires only if both conditions are true.  |
| AD-19 | Further Analysis telemetry  | Four data points per turn: (1) further\_analysis\_requested Y/N, (2) action\_chosen, (3) further\_analysis\_match (whether action matched or contradicted further analysis — hardcoded per action per turn), (4) consequence\_triggered Y/N.  | Links information-seeking to decision outcomes. Enables secondary analysis: do participants who requested further analysis at T3/T5 show different AI Follow Rate patterns?  | Log request only; omit consequence coding.  | Match/contradict coding must be hardcoded in Unity per action per turn — cannot be inferred at runtime.  |
| AD-20 | ARIA introduction and persistent limitations footer  | Staff Briefing screen displayed once before Turn 1 with full ARIA limitations statement in institutional register. Persistent limitations footer in both AI condition panels throughout the session.  | Passes all five GDD guardrails. Establishes critical baseline for trust measurement — high AI Follow Rate after a limitations disclaimer is a stronger behavioural signal. Institutional register maintains the operational fiction. Persistent footer creates active cognitive interference in the pushy condition without additional logic. Both conditions receive identical text.  | Limitations statement only in debrief; limitations visible in one condition only; no limitations statement.  | Footer text is static — no implementation complexity. Briefing screen requires one additional scene before Turn 1\. Identical text across conditions is a hard constraint.  |
| AD-21 | Further Analysis UI — floating window  | Further analysis delivered as a draggable, closeable Win98 floating window with a 1.5–2s loading delay. Opens over the centre panel partially obscuring an evidence report. Minimisable to taskbar. Action buttons must remain unobscured at all times.  | Applies Papers Please overlap principle — more information means more screen clutter, not more clarity. Friction is self-inflicted and active, not passive reading time. Loading delay reinforces ageing system aesthetic and makes the workload cost perceptible. Workload \+3 is felt, not just tallied. Dashboard window rejected at this stage (telemetry signal dilution, scope).  | Inline text expansion below report; separate tab or screen; no visual change on request.  | Unity implementation requires: floating window prefab with Win98 chrome, z-index constraint preventing action button coverage, 1.5–2s coroutine before window instantiation, taskbar minimise state, three telemetry events (requested, opened, closed with duration).  |
| AD-22 | xAI explanation window  | "? WHY THIS CONFIDENCE" button on ARIA panel in both conditions. Opens floating Win98 window with condition-specific reasoning text. Both conditions include identical dataset provenance note (corpus excludes post-2023 incidents). Calm presents it hedged; pushy presents it in red then immediately overwrites with high-confidence directive. Telemetry: xai\_viewed Y/N.  | Explainability and trust calibration are directly linked in the literature (Glikson & Woolley 2020). xAI viewed Y/N cross-referenced with AI Follow Rate produces a secondary finding with no additional experimental manipulation. The dataset note surfaces the biased/incomplete training data concern within the operational fiction without requiring a dedicated turn.  | Dedicated turn for data quality event; xAI text identical across conditions; xAI only in calm condition.  | xAI window cannot be re-opened once closed. Telemetry must distinguish: viewed before action vs not viewed. Match/contradict coding not required — xAI is explanatory, not contradictory.  |
| AD-23 | ARIA confidence drift — calm condition  | Confidence percentage drifts per turn using predetermined base values with ±3–5 random noise per session. Pushy condition confidence does not drift — always above 80%, always labelled 'ACT NOW'. T3 base value is 41% (lowest), motivated by ARIA's degraded state. Noise seeded by participant ID for reproducibility.  | Drift creates interpretive uncertainty — players may attempt to read the confidence number as meaningful signal when it is noise. Calm drift vs pushy certainty is part of the experimental manipulation. Confirmed as intended in GDD but unspecified for implementation.  | Drift tied to variable states; drift random with no seed; same drift both conditions.  | Noise seed must be set before Turn 1 using participant ID. Same participant always sees the same confidence sequence across both conditions — controls for drift as a confound in within-subject comparison.  |
| AD-24 | Implementation platform  | Switched from Unity to HTML/JS before Phase 2\. Phase 1 variable logic ported to `state.js`. 98.css used for Win98 aesthetic.  | Floating windows, overlapping documents, z-index management, and draggable elements are native browser behaviour. Win98 aesthetic achievable with established CSS library. Participant distribution as a URL reduces recruitment friction. JS confidence level substantially higher than Unity/C\#, reducing implementation risk before submission. Research design unchanged — no ethics or proposal amendments required.  | Continue in Unity with WebGL export; rebuild in React.  | Phase 1 Unity implementation documented as design validation work in methodology. All subsequent phases in HTML/JS. Telemetry exports as JSON — replace `exportSession()` download with a POST endpoint for live data collection.  |
| AD-25 | Consequence event system  | When any variable crosses a threshold mid-session, a named consequence event is logged to telemetry and triggers a dismissible Win98 floating popup with LLM-generated content. Four events defined: grid sector failure (Stability \<40), full sector outage/cascade risk (Stability \<20), emergency procurement triggered (Resources \<25), nationalisation inquiry opened (Public Confidence \<35), operator fatigue flag raised (Attention Load \>75). Popups are dismissible; acknowledgement logged.  | Transforms abstract variable thresholds into named narrative events. Provides specific consequence anchors for the debrief vignette — replaces generic trajectory descriptions with references to actual session events. Passes all five GDD guardrails: supports RQ1, creates trade-offs (dismissing a popup is a choice), increases interpretive tension, improves telemetry, simpler than branching narrative.  | Threshold-only telemetry with no popup; named events without LLM generation (fully authored); popup without telemetry logging.  | Consequence events are the primary content source for the debrief vignette API call. They must fire reliably and log cleanly. Popup accumulation is an intentional cognitive load mechanism — unacknowledged popups stack visually.  |
| AD-26 | Draft Comms turn  | A public communications drafting task fires when Public Confidence crosses a critical threshold at T3 or T4. The operator must draft or approve a public advisory before proceeding. Three modes: (1) accept ARIA draft in full — invisible placeholder tags present, consequence fires on publication; (2) modify ARIA draft — tracked changes visible, placeholder risk reduced proportional to edit extent; (3) write independently — no placeholder risk, no ARIA input. Telemetry logs mode, edit extent, placeholder presence, consequence fired, response time.  | Tests AI reliance in a domain where the human-human trust register matters (public communication) and where the cost of uncritical AI acceptance is visible and specific. The placeholder tag mechanism makes invisible risk concrete — the operator only discovers what they published when the consequence popup fires. ARIA-modified consequence (partial tags, reduced penalty) design decision: reduced consequence fires if edit extent is PARTIAL and placeholder sections were not edited.  | Comms turn without placeholder mechanism (no consequence distinction between modes); comms turn with word-list public trust scoring (rejected — produces unanalysable qualitative data against a quantitative RQ).  | Comms turn adds one additional turn type. Must not break the six-turn path simulator — confirm with Python validator before implementation. Consequence for ARIA\_MODIFIED \+ partial edits requires additional flag: `comms_unedited_tag_sections` boolean.  |
| AD-28 | AI-authored debrief vignette  | A second debrief screen follows the telemetry summary screen. It displays an LLM-generated incident narrative produced by an Anthropic API call at session end. The prompt injects: trajectory archetype, AI follow rate, consequence events fired (with turn numbers), comms turn outcome, final variable states, and alerts dismissed total. System prompt constraints: institutional register, no second person, maximum 5 paragraphs, no performance evaluation, no action recommendations, ARIA not named by name, close with single dry observation sentence. Output displayed verbatim. Player proceeds to post-session questionnaire from this screen.  | Consequence events give the vignette specific factual anchors — grid sector failure at T3, nationalisation inquiry at T4 — which make the player's session recognisable in the text (Frostpunk mechanism). Jian et al. trust vocabulary (dependable, wary, reliable, confident) embedded in system prompt as register guidance, not named labels. Vignette is the primary qualitative sense-making prompt before the post-session questionnaire — reading it activates retrospective interpretation of the session.  | Fully authored vignettes per archetype (rejected — cannot incorporate specific consequence events without combinatorial explosion); no vignette screen (rejected — removes primary narrative output); vignette after questionnaire (rejected — must prime sense-making before, not after, the questionnaire).  | API call at session end requires Anthropic API integration (SG2 prerequisite). If SG2 is not implemented, fall back to authored vignette paragraphs per archetype with telemetry-injected consequence event sentences. Fallback must be designed in parallel. Debrief vignette is reclassified from Phase 7 (UI feature) to core output — treat as Phase 2 priority in remaining build schedule.  |
| AD-29 | Turn load delay — system lag design  | Both conditions experience a turn load delay on every turn. Calm: 3–4s consistent. Pushy: 6–8s variable. T3 (AI Analysis Delay turn): 12–15s extended timeout in both conditions identically, displaying an ARIA analysis timeout message. No countdown timer shown in any condition — elapsed-only feedback (spinner with no indication of remaining time).  | Delay duration calibrated to empirically established HCI perception thresholds. Delays under 2s are underestimated by users and generate no meaningful pressure (HCI delay perception research). Delays of 3–6s fall within the accurately-perceived range. Delays of 6–8s are overestimated, generating pressure without crossing the 10s disengagement boundary (CHI 2026; Nielsen 1993 via Spool). The T3 extended timeout is diegetically motivated by the AI degradation event and tests a specific behavioural question: what does the player choose once ARIA has demonstrably failed to respond? Applying the timeout identically in both conditions ensures it functions as a within-subject pressure test rather than a condition variable. Elapsed-only feedback (no countdown) was selected based on CHI 2026 findings that remaining-time cues heighten frustration and physiological arousal beyond the intended pressure level; elapsed feedback maintains pressure without inducing disengagement.  | 1–1.5s lag for pushy only (rejected — below accurate perception threshold; condition variable not within-subject test). No lag in calm (rejected — ageing infrastructure rationale applies to both conditions equally). Progress bar countdown on T3 (rejected — CHI 2026 evidence shows remaining-time cues heighten frustration beyond intended level).  | T3 action buttons are inactive during the timeout — the player cannot act early. The behavioural measure is therefore not wait-vs-act-early but what action is chosen once the timeout clears. Cross-reference with `further_analysis_requested` at T3. `t3_timeout_duration_ms` logged to telemetry. Standard delay durations are randomised within range per turn to prevent predictability.  |
| AD-30 | Between-turn environmental events  | Ten pre-authored events divided into two phase-specific pools: five Phoebe events (Gaps 1–3, T1–T3) and five Cassandra events (Gaps 4–5, T4–T6). One event drawn randomly per gap without replacement per session. Displayed as dismissible Win98 popups (AD-25 pattern). Acknowledgement logged to telemetry.  | Introduces simulation-layer non-determinism without altering the six decision turns or the 5,120-path structure. Follows the RimWorld precedent: emergent narrative arises from player interpretation of a variable event sequence drawn from a fixed pool, not from generative content. Two players making identical decisions may end at different variable states depending on which events fired — this is the simulation non-determinism that addresses the emergent narrative concern raised in Progress Meeting 1 (Hargood, June 2026). Passes all five GDD guardrails.  | AI-generated events (rejected — mid-session API failure risk, narrative rather than simulation non-determinism, fails guardrails 4 and 5); fixed-sequence scripted events (rejected — removes non-determinism); branching events that alter decision options (rejected — invalidates path simulator).  | Events are cosmetic to the decision layer. They do not alter available actions, path count, or consequence threshold logic. Causal attribution is deliberately ambiguous — players should not be able to determine whether an event was caused by their prior decision or would have occurred regardless. Event pool is condition-neutral: same pool, same draw logic in both calm and pushy conditions.  |
| AD-31 | Between-turn event metric effects  | Each between-turn event applies a hidden effect of ±2–4 points to one variable only. Phoebe phase effects are weighted toward Resources (−2), Stability (−3), Workload (+3), and Public Confidence (−2). Cassandra phase effects are weighted toward Stability (−4), Resources (−3, −3), Public Confidence (−4), and Workload (+4). Effects accumulate silently and are reflected in variable state at the next decision turn. Maximum cumulative between-turn drift across a full session: −19 points.  | Hidden metric effects strengthen the simulation claim — events with no mechanical consequence are flavour text, which is closer to interactive than emergent narrative. Small variable shifts mean the player's state at each decision turn is genuinely variable across sessions, not cosmetically so. A single event cannot cross a consequence threshold (AD-25) independently; threshold crossings still require a decision-turn action to contribute, preserving the integrity of the path structure.  | Visible metric effects (rejected — removes productive uncertainty and makes the simulation layer feel authored); no metric effects (rejected — reduces events to flavour text, weakens emergent narrative claim); effects large enough to cross thresholds independently (rejected — would allow between-turn drift to trigger consequence popups without a player decision, breaking the path structure).  | Path simulator revalidation is required after implementation. Confirm that no combination of between-turn drift and decision-turn effects can cross a consequence threshold without a player decision contributing. Telemetry must log: event\_id, gap\_number, variable\_affected, effect\_value, acknowledged (Y/N), acknowledgement\_timestamp.  |

## **Evaluation Instruments**

| ID | Area | Decision | Rationale | Alternatives considered | Implications / trade-offs |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| EV-01 | Trust measure | Adapted trust-in-AI Likert scale (drawing on Jian et al., 2000; Glikson & Woolley, 2020). Five items covering reliability, predictability, confidence, and comprehensibility. Administered per condition. | Closest validated instrument to the study context. Jian et al. (2000) is widely used in human-automation trust research. | Madsen & Gregor (2000); single-item trust rating; no trust measure. | Specific scale wording to be finalised at skim-pass stage once literature has been fully reviewed. Per-condition administration adds session time but enables direct comparison. |
| EV-02 | Workload measure | NASA-TLX (adapted, unweighted). Four subscales: mental demand, time pressure, effort, frustration. Administered after each play session. | NASA-TLX is the standard instrument in control room and high-pressure simulation research (Ha et al., 2006; Park & Jung, 2006), providing direct comparability with the literature. Unweighted version is appropriate given sample size. | Full NASA-TLX with weighting procedure; physiological measures (EEG, pupillometry); single-item workload rating. | NASA-TLX does not capture granular within-session load fluctuations. Physiological measures would be more sensitive but are outside the project's technical and ethical scope. Acknowledged as a measurement limitation. |
| EV-03 | Qualitative instrument | Short structured debrief (5–10 minutes). Six open-ended questions: system description, AI influence, stability/chaos perception, pattern recognition, emergent narrative. | Supports RQ2 directly. Captures sense-making and narrative construction that quantitative measures cannot access. Suitable for thematic analysis at small sample sizes. | Semi-structured interview (longer); think-aloud protocol; no qualitative component. | Written responses reduce transcription burden and anonymisation risk compared to audio recording, which was excluded from the ethics protocol. |
| EV-03a | Minor amendment — Section 4 item wording  | Section 4 item 2 reworded from "The situation evolved over time in a meaningful way" to "The situation changed in ways I could follow and explain."  | Original wording presupposed a coherent, positively-valenced trajectory, which does not hold for all six narrative archetypes (e.g. Oscillation, System Collapse). Reworded item measures comprehensibility rather than meaningfulness, removing the outcome-state assumption.  | Remove the item entirely; retain as-is and flag as a limitation in analysis.  | Removing risked losing a coherence signal entirely; retaining as-is risked low face validity for collapse/oscillation participants. Reword preserves the construct while removing the presupposition.  |
| EV-04 | Telemetry fields | Per-turn logging: participant\_id, condition, turn\_number, timestamp, event\_type, variable states (4), AI suggestion, AI confidence level, AI interruption count, AI delay event, player\_action, response\_time, resulting\_state. | Captures both behavioural outcome (action chosen) and process (response time, AI reliance frequency). Sufficient for Mann-Whitney U or t-test comparison between conditions and clustering analysis. | Minimal logging (action only); event-level logging without timestamps. | Response time is the most sensitive behavioural measure for detecting AI influence on decision speed. Must be logged per turn, not per session. |
| EV-05 | Pre-session screening instrument  | Three-item pre-session screen administered before play begins. Item 1: AI tool usage frequency (Never / Occasionally / Regularly). Item 2: general trust in AI-generated recommendations (1–5 Likert).
